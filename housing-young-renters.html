CASE STUDY · HOUSING · EQUITY

Who the housing squeeze really hits

2021 Census · Statistics Canada · Tools: Python (pandas), Tableau, HTML/SVG

"Housing is unaffordable for young people" is a familiar headline. I wanted to test whether the data says something more specific — and more useful for policy — than that.

The question

Affordability debates usually split the population into renters vs. owners, or young vs. old. But those cuts are blunt. If you were designing support for renters, which renters would you target first? I set out to find where cost burden actually concentrates, using a definition I could hold constant: Statistics Canada counts a household as being in "unaffordable housing" when it spends more than 30% of pre-tax income on shelter.

The approach

I worked from Statistics Canada's 2021 Census study A tale of two renters, which distinguishes recent renters (households that moved into their home within the past year) from existing renters. My steps:

  • Anchored on one consistent metric (the >30% shelter-cost-to-income ratio) so every comparison was apples-to-apples.
  • Compared recent vs. existing renters overall, then segmented by age band to see whether the gap was uniform or concentrated.
  • Built a derived "entry penalty" — the percentage-point gap between recent and existing renters at each age — to isolate the effect of just having entered the market.
  • Visualized it as a three-step argument (headline → age breakdown → isolated penalty) so the conclusion is legible at a glance, and verified every figure against the source.

The finding

The squeeze isn't really "young renters" as a category — it's renters at the moment of entry, and that moment is hardest for the youngest.

43.2% of recent renters were cost-burdened vs. 30.5% of existing renters. Among renters aged 18–24, the split widens to 45.0% vs. 23.0% — recent renters are cost-burdened at nearly double the rate of established renters the same age. The "entry penalty" peaks at 22 percentage points for 18–24 and narrows with age.

The mechanism is intuitive once segmented: people signing their first leases pay today's market rents with the least savings to absorb them, while established tenants are partly shielded by slower increases on ongoing tenancies. The age pattern falls straight out of that.

Why it matters

The "so what" is a targeting insight. Support framed broadly around "renters" spreads thin and misses the sharpest pressure point; the data argues for help aimed at the transition into the market — first-lease and young-renter support — where the burden is most acute. It's also a reminder of the method that runs through my work: an aggregate ("renters are cost-burdened") can hide the segment that actually needs the intervention. Segment before you conclude.
