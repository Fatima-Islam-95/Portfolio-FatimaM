How one cancer statistic gets misread

Between 2000 and 2011 Canada's crude cancer death rate rose — which looks like losing ground. But the death rate fell within every age group. The crude rate rose only because the population got older. Correcting for age (the age-standardized rate) shows cancer mortality actually falling.

Takeaway: Before reading a rising health rate as "things are getting worse," check whether it is crude or age-standardized. In an aging country, crude rates of anything age-related drift upward even when the underlying risk is dropping. For comparisons across years, regions, or countries, the age-standardized rate is the honest one.
Sources: Statistics Canada, Age-standardized Rates (panels 1–3, 1991 standard population); Public Health Agency of Canada / Canadian Cancer Statistics and Statistics Canada Canadian Vital Statistics — Death Database (panel 4, 2011 standard population, anchors 1992 = 247 & 2013 = 200).
